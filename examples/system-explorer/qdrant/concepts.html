Core Concepts

The Building Blocks of Qdrant

Qdrant is built on eight core concepts. Understanding them gives you the vocabulary to reason about how vector similarity search works, how to configure Qdrant for your workload, and how to get the most out of its capabilities.

📍

Vectors & Embeddings

A vector (or embedding) is a list of floating-point numbers that represents a piece of data — a sentence, an image, a product — in a high-dimensional space. These vectors are produced by machine learning models that learn to place similar items close together.

💡
Analogy: Imagine every book in a library placed on a vast map where proximity represents similarity. Cookbooks cluster near each other, sci-fi novels group together, and a cookbook about science fiction cuisine sits between the two clusters. Vectors are the GPS coordinates on this map.

Why it matters: Vectors are the fundamental data type in Qdrant. Every search query starts with a vector, and Qdrant's job is to find the stored vectors closest to it. The quality of your results depends on your embedding model, but Qdrant ensures retrieval is fast and accurate.

📌

Points

A point is the atomic unit of data in Qdrant. Each point consists of a unique identifier (64-bit integer or UUID), one or more vectors, and an optional JSON payload containing metadata.

💡
Analogy: Think of a point as a library card. The ID is the card number, the vector is the "location on the similarity map" where the book belongs, and the payload is all the extra information — title, author, genre, year.

Why it matters: Points are the records you interact with through the API. When you upsert data, you create points. When you search, Qdrant returns points whose vectors are most similar to your query. The payload on each point enables rich filtering without a separate metadata database.

📁

Collections

A collection is a named group of points that share the same vector configuration — dimensionality and distance metric. All vectors within a collection must have the same number of dimensions and are compared using the same similarity function.

💡
Analogy: A collection is like a filing cabinet where every folder uses the same labeling system. You would not mix Dewey Decimal folders with Library of Congress folders — similarly, you would not mix 384-dimensional vectors with 1536-dimensional vectors in the same collection.

Why it matters: Collections define the schema for your vector data. When you create one, you choose the vector size (e.g., 1536 for OpenAI embeddings) and the distance metric (cosine, dot product, Euclidean, or Manhattan). This is an immutable choice that determines how similarity is calculated.

📋

Payloads

A payload is arbitrary JSON metadata attached to a point. Payloads can contain strings, numbers, booleans, arrays, nested objects, geo-coordinates, and datetime values. Payload fields can be indexed for efficient filtering during search.

💡
Analogy: If a vector tells you where an item sits on the similarity map, the payload is the item's detailed profile card — its category, price, date, language, and any other structured attribute you want to filter on.

Why it matters: Payloads enable filtered vector search — one of Qdrant's standout features. Instead of finding the 10 most similar items globally, you can find the 10 most similar items that are also in the "electronics" category and priced under $50.

🕸️

HNSW Index

Hierarchical Navigable Small World (HNSW) is the primary indexing algorithm for approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search. It builds a multi-layered graph where upper layers have few nodes with long-range connections and lower layers contain all nodes with short-range connections.

💡
Analogy: Imagine navigating a city to find the closest coffee shop. At the highway level (top layer), you quickly drive to the right neighborhood. At the street level, you navigate to the right block. At the sidewalk level (bottom layer), you walk to the exact shop. HNSW does this with vectors.

Why it matters: Brute-force comparison against millions of vectors would be too slow. HNSW provides sub-linear search time — typically O(log n) — while maintaining high recall (95-99%+). Key parameters are m (edges per node, default 16) and ef (search beam width).

📦

Quantization

Quantization compresses vector representations to reduce memory usage and speed up distance calculations. Qdrant supports Scalar (float32 to int8, 4x compression), Binary (float32 to 1-bit, 32x compression), and Product Quantization (up to 64x compression).

💡
Analogy: Like reducing image resolution. A 4K photo (float32) is detailed but huge. A compressed JPEG (scalar quantization) looks almost identical at a fraction of the size. A thumbnail (binary quantization) is much smaller — you can scan through thousands quickly, then double-check the full image for your top picks.

Why it matters: At scale, vector storage dominates memory costs. 10M vectors at 1536 dimensions = ~57 GB in float32. With scalar quantization, ~14 GB. With binary, ~1.8 GB. Qdrant's rescoring mechanism re-evaluates top candidates with original vectors to maintain high accuracy.

🔍

Filtering

Filtering applies structured conditions on payload fields during vector search. Conditions include keyword match, numeric range, geo-radius, full-text search, datetime ranges, and nested fields, combinable with must, should, and must_not boolean logic.

💡
Analogy: Filtering is like adding WHERE clauses to a SQL query. Vector search alone is "find me similar items." Filtered vector search is "find me similar items WHERE category = 'shoes' AND price < 100."

Why it matters: Filters are integrated into the HNSW traversal (not post-processing), so filtering does not significantly degrade search performance. This is critical for real-world applications where results must satisfy both semantic similarity and business constraints.

📏

Distance Metrics

A distance metric defines how similarity between two vectors is calculated. Qdrant supports Cosine (angular similarity), Dot Product (direction + magnitude), Euclidean (straight-line distance), and Manhattan (grid distance).

💡
Analogy: Choosing a distance metric is like choosing how to measure the "closeness" of two cities. Euclidean is straight-line (as the crow flies). Manhattan follows the road grid. Cosine measures the angle of the direction, ignoring how far away the cities are.

Why it matters: The metric must match your embedding model's training objective. Most text embedding models work best with cosine similarity. Choosing the wrong metric will produce poor results even with a good model.
